Section 01 ‚Äî The Word "Vipassana"
Q: What does the word "Vipassana" actually mean?
The word comes from the ancient Pali language ‚Äî the language in which the Buddha originally taught. It is made of two parts. "Vi" means special, clear, or deep. "Passana" means seeing or observing. Together ‚Äî Vipassana means to see clearly. To see things as they actually are ‚Äî not as we wish them to be, not as we fear them to be, but as they actually are. That is the entire practice in three words: See. Clearly. Reality.
Q: Why Pali specifically? Why not Sanskrit?
Because the Buddha taught in Pali ‚Äî the common language of ordinary people in northern India around 500 BCE. It was not the language of priests or scholars. Sanskrit was for the elite. Pali was for the farmer, the trader, the mother, the child. This choice was itself a philosophical statement ‚Äî that the truth he taught belonged to everyone, not only to the privileged. Goenka honoured this same spirit.
Q: "To see things as they really are" ‚Äî what makes it so difficult?
Because we almost never do it. From birth, every experience is immediately filtered through memory, expectation, desire, and fear. We don't see what is in front of us ‚Äî we see our interpretation of it. Vipassana removes those distortions slowly ‚Äî not by thinking about them but by observing raw physical sensation before the mind has filtered it. To see things as they really are is the most radical act a human being can attempt ‚Äî because it includes seeing yourself as you really are.
Q: How would you explain Vipassana to a ten-year-old?
You know how sometimes you get very angry ‚Äî and later you think: why did I say that? The feeling came so fast, and the action came so fast after it, that there was no space in between to choose. Vipassana is the practice of making that space bigger. It teaches you to notice ‚Äî very slowly ‚Äî what is actually happening inside you before you do anything. When you can feel the anger before it becomes the shout, you suddenly have a choice. That is a skill. And like all skills, it can be learned.
Q: After everything you have experienced ‚Äî what does the word Vipassana mean to YOU now?
Before the course ‚Äî Vipassana meant a technique. I understood the words "to see things as they really are" the way you understand a sentence in a language you have studied but never spoken. After the course ‚Äî and three years of daily practice, imperfect and ongoing ‚Äî Vipassana means the choice I have that I did not have before. The two seconds between what happens and what I do. The ability to feel fear fully and act anyway. It means the mosquito I did not slap. It means my father's tired face, seen clearly for the first time on Day 9. It means the 4am darkness and the decision ‚Äî renewed every morning ‚Äî to sit down and look honestly at what is actually here. It means ‚Äî to see clearly. And in that seeing, however partial, however imperfect ‚Äî something has been irrevocably changed.
Section 02 ‚Äî What Is Vipassana: Basics & Philosophy
Q: What is Vipassana and how is it different from other meditation?
Vipassana means "to see things as they really are." It is one of India's most ancient meditation techniques, rediscovered by Gautama Buddha and preserved in its original purity in Myanmar. Unlike concentration-based meditation like mantra chanting or visualization, Vipassana works on deep-rooted mental conditioning by observing bodily sensations with equanimity. No visualization, no deity, no mantra ‚Äî just pure self-observation. Before Vipassana, I tried breath-awareness on my own. It calmed me temporarily, but the moment stress arose, the calm vanished. With Vipassana, there is now a gap ‚Äî however small ‚Äî between what happens and how I respond. That gap is what Vipassana builds.
Q: Is Vipassana a religious practice?
No. Goenka made it clear from Day 1 ‚Äî this technique belongs to no religion. You are not asked to believe anything. You are only asked to observe. I have met devout Hindus, practicing Muslims, and committed atheists ‚Äî all sitting in the same hall, all benefiting equally. That universality is not a marketing claim ‚Äî I witnessed it personally. Vipassana strengthened my own values without imposing any external belief system on me.
Q: What are Anicca, Dukkha, and Anatta?
Anicca means impermanence. Dukkha means suffering arising from clinging to what is impermanent. Anatta means no fixed self ‚Äî what we call "I" is a constantly changing process. On Day 8, scanning sensations, I noticed a mild tingling in my left hand. Over ten minutes, I watched it intensify ‚Äî almost electric. Then, without any intervention from me, it began to dissolve. Within another ten minutes it had faded entirely. I had done nothing to create it and nothing to stop it. In that sequence, Anicca was not a philosophical statement. It was this ‚Äî a vivid, undeniable demonstration that nothing stays.
Q: What is Sankara in Vipassana?
Sankara roughly means mental formation or conditioned reaction. Every time we experience something pleasant, we generate craving. Every time unpleasant, we generate aversion. These reactions, when not observed and allowed to pass, leave a residue ‚Äî a Sankara ‚Äî in the subconscious. Accumulated over a lifetime, these form the deep conditioning that drives habitual behavior. This explained why I kept repeating the same mistakes despite knowing better intellectually. The Sankaras are at a deeper level than intellect. Only experiential observation at the level of body sensation can reach and dissolve them.
Q: Who was S.N. Goenka and what was his contribution?
S.N. Goenka (1924‚Äì2013) was a Burmese-Indian teacher who brought Vipassana from Myanmar to India in 1969 after learning it from Sayagyi U Ba Khin. His greatest contribution was making it universally accessible ‚Äî free of cost, free of religion, free of dogma. Today there are over 200 Vipassana centers worldwide. He introduced Vipassana into Tihar Jail with remarkable results. What personally moved me was his intellectual honesty. He would say repeatedly ‚Äî "Don't accept what I say. Experience it yourself and then judge." He never built a personality cult. He often said he was just a messenger ‚Äî the technique was the teacher.
Section 03 ‚Äî Your Personal Journey
Q: Why specifically Vipassana? Why not other styles of meditation?
What drew me to Vipassana specifically was its complete rationality and lack of dogma. No visualization, no deity, no mantra ‚Äî just pure self-observation. It appealed to my scientific temperament. I wasn't asked to believe anything ‚Äî I was asked to observe and experience. I had read about the Tihar Jail experiment where thousands of prisoners practiced Vipassana and showed measurable changes in behavior. That practical, verifiable impact in such a tough environment convinced me this was something real ‚Äî not just theory.
Q: What was your favourite lesson or teaching of Goenka?
"Anicca, Anicca, Anicca." Everything is impermanent. He would say this not as a philosophical statement but almost as a practical instruction for how to live. When you are happy, you don't cling desperately ‚Äî you know it will change. When you are suffering, you don't despair ‚Äî you know this too shall pass. I remember a particularly difficult sitting where my knees were in tremendous pain. Goenka's recorded voice said ‚Äî "Anicca... observe... it will pass." And it did. Not immediately, but my relationship with the pain changed. I carry this into daily life as a practical tool, not just a philosophical idea.
Q: What do you attain from Vipassana?
The most significant thing Vipassana has given me is the ability to pause. Before, if something went wrong, my reaction was almost automatic. Now there is a small but real gap between what happens and how I respond. In that gap, I can choose. Second ‚Äî much clearer awareness of my own patterns: where I get impatient, where I become defensive, what triggers anxiety. Third ‚Äî the ability to sit with discomfort without immediately escaping it. In a forest posting, where conditions can be harsh and isolation real, this capacity is not a small thing. Vipassana didn't give me peace as a feeling ‚Äî it gave me tools to create peace from within.
Q: You are young ‚Äî isn't Vipassana something for older people?
The earlier you begin, the less conditioning you have to undo. A young person beginning practice has perhaps twenty-five years of conditioning accumulated. An older person may have sixty or seventy. Both can make progress, but the young person begins with a lighter burden. Also ‚Äî there is no age at which suffering is inappropriate to address. The reactive patterns that will define a career in public service are being established right now. Beginning the practice while those patterns are being formed is far more useful than waiting until they are fully entrenched. The Buddha achieved enlightenment at thirty-five. The Gita was delivered to Arjuna on a battlefield ‚Äî not in a retirement home.
Section 04 ‚Äî Anapana, Vipassana & Metta System
Q: What exactly is Anapana meditation?
Anapana is the first stage of Vipassana practice. "Ana" means incoming breath, "Apana" means outgoing breath. You sit, close your eyes, and observe the breath entering and leaving through the nostrils ‚Äî specifically the sensation of air touching the area below the nostrils and above the upper lip. That small area becomes your entire universe of focus. From my own experience, it sounds deceptively simple ‚Äî but it is extraordinarily difficult. Within seconds the mind wanders. The moment you realize you have wandered, simply return to the breath without judgment. That returning, done thousands of times, is itself the training.
Q: How is Anapana different from Pranayama?
Pranayama is breath control ‚Äî you deliberately alter the rhythm, depth, and pattern of breathing. Anapana is fundamentally different ‚Äî you do not control the breath at all. You simply observe it as it is, however it is. This carries philosophical weight. Pranayama is doing ‚Äî Anapana is pure observing. From the very beginning, through Anapana, you are training the mind's most essential skill ‚Äî to see without distorting what it sees. That quality of non-interfering awareness is what the entire Vipassana path depends on.
Q: What is Metta Bhavana and when is it practiced?
Metta Bhavana means cultivation of loving-kindness or goodwill toward all beings. It is practiced on Day 10 ‚Äî the final day. After 9 days of intensive inner observation, Metta serves as a beautiful culmination. You generate goodwill ‚Äî first toward yourself, then toward those close to you, then toward neutral people, then toward difficult people, and finally toward all beings everywhere without exception. My experience of Metta on Day 10 was genuinely moving. After 9 days of mostly difficult inner work, sitting in that hall genuinely wishing happiness to every being ‚Äî including people I had conflict with ‚Äî felt profoundly natural rather than forced. The ground had been prepared by 9 days of clearing.
Q: How do Anapana, Vipassana, and Metta form a complete system?
Goenka described it as a three-part architecture ‚Äî each part essential, each depending on the others. Anapana develops Samadhi ‚Äî concentration. Without this, the mind is too scattered to do the deeper work. Vipassana uses that sharpened mind to develop Prajna ‚Äî wisdom through experiential understanding of impermanence. This is where deep conditioning begins to dissolve. Metta is the natural expression of a purified mind ‚Äî when craving and aversion reduce, genuine warmth toward all life arises naturally. Together they form the complete path ‚Äî concentration to see clearly, wisdom to understand deeply, compassion to live rightly.

Q: 4am wake-up every day ‚Äî be honest about what that was actually like.
Day 1 ‚Äî the bell rang and I lay there for a full minute not believing it was already time. Four hours of sleep. Cold, dark, completely silent room. I got up ‚Äî not from virtue, but from knowing that lying in bed thinking about getting up is actually more exhausting than getting up. Day 3 ‚Äî I woke approximately two minutes before the bell. Three days of waking at 4am had begun to reset something in the body. Day 4 onward ‚Äî the waking became progressively less difficult. Not pleasant ‚Äî it never became pleasant. But the resistance reduced. I now wake between 4:30 and 5am at home ‚Äî the shift in my body's sense of when a day should begin has never fully reversed.
Q: What happened on Day 4 when Vipassana was introduced?
Day 4 was the most significant transition. The technique: systematic body scanning from the top of the head to the tips of the toes and back again, observing whatever sensations are present at each point without reacting. The Day 4 discourse explained it beautifully: the body is the field where the mind's conditioned patterns are stored. Every mental reaction has a physical component. Craving manifests as a pleasant sensation you want to prolong. Aversion manifests as an unpleasant sensation you want to end. By observing these without reacting, you break the chain at its root. Goenka used a beautiful analogy: the mind is like a pot of boiling water. You can blow on it from the top ‚Äî that is intellectual understanding. Or you can remove the fire from underneath ‚Äî that is what Vipassana does.
Q: Which of Goenka's analogies stayed with you most?
The analogy of the two arrows. When an unpleasant experience occurs, two arrows strike you. The first arrow is the experience itself ‚Äî the pain, the loss, the failure, the insult. Often outside your control. The second arrow is the mental reaction ‚Äî the self-pity, the anger, the rumination. The second arrow is always self-inflicted. Ordinary human beings feel the first arrow and immediately pick up the second and shoot themselves with it. Vipassana does not remove the first arrow. Life will always have first arrows. But it trains you to put down the second arrow ‚Äî to feel the first fully, without immediately picking up another. For a forest officer ‚Äî a difficult posting is a first arrow. The mental narrative "this is unfair, I deserved better" ‚Äî those are second and third arrows. All self-inflicted. All avoidable.
Section 06 ‚Äî Deep Personal Experiences
Q: Take us through your very first day at the Vipassana center.
The first day was a mix of excitement and quiet panic. When I arrived, we surrendered our phones, books, journals ‚Äî everything. I remember handing over my phone and feeling a strange anxiety, almost like losing a limb. I hadn't realized until that moment how attached I was to it ‚Äî not for calls, but as an escape from my own mind. That realization itself was the first lesson, and I hadn't even sat down to meditate yet. That night in my room, without a phone, without anyone to talk to, I lay awake for a long time. My mind was extraordinarily loud. I realized that in ordinary life, I had never actually been alone with my mind. There was always something filling the space. That first night showed me how much noise was living inside me that I had been successfully avoiding for years.
Q: What was the single hardest moment during your course?
Day 3. Without question. The initial curiosity had worn off. My knees ached constantly. My back was stiff. My mind was still extremely restless. I could not see any progress and was not even halfway through. I remember sitting in the hall that afternoon, genuinely asking myself ‚Äî what am I doing here? What stopped me was something very simple ‚Äî I decided to just finish the current sitting. Not the whole course, just the next hour. That reduction of the horizon ‚Äî from "ten days" to "this one hour" ‚Äî was the only thing that kept me there. That evening, Goenka's discourse addressed exactly this ‚Äî he said Day 3 is when most people want to leave. The crisis point that, if crossed, opens into genuine progress.
Q: Did you have a breakthrough moment? Describe it.
Yes, on Day 6. About 30 minutes into a strong determination sitting, I became aware of sensations throughout my entire body simultaneously ‚Äî not pain, not pressure, but a kind of fine, subtle vibration. My breathing had slowed almost to nothing. My mind was unusually still ‚Äî not blank, but like a very calm lake rather than the choppy ocean it usually was. Walking out of the hall afterward, the trees looked different ‚Äî not literally, but in how I perceived them. Colors more vivid. Bird sounds distinct and clear. I felt strangely unhurried. Before that moment, I was practicing on faith. After it, I was practicing from experience. That is the difference that mattered.
Q: How did you deal with physical pain during sitting?
Around Day 5, instead of fighting the pain, I tried observing it. I noticed where exactly it was ‚Äî not "my knee hurts" as a vague complaint, but precisely ‚Äî there is a burning sensation approximately two inches below the left kneecap. Is it constant? No ‚Äî it pulses. Is it solid? No ‚Äî within the pain there are different qualities. This act of precise observation did something unexpected ‚Äî the pain became interesting rather than threatening. Not pleasant, but the suffering ‚Äî which is the mental layer added on top of the physical sensation ‚Äî reduced dramatically. I understood the difference between pain and suffering. Pain is the sensation. Suffering is the resistance to the sensation. Vipassana dissolves the resistance.
Section 07 ‚Äî Post-Course Real Life
Q: Give a specific real-life example where Vipassana made a real difference.
In a group study session, someone made a dismissive comment about my preparation in front of others in a way that felt genuinely humiliating. My immediate internal response was intense ‚Äî heat rising in my face, tightening in my chest, the strong impulse to retaliate sharply. What happened instead was a trained reflex. I noticed the physical sensation of embarrassment. Heat in the face, tightness in the throat. Just noticed it, without adding the story "this is terrible, they all think I am incompetent." That observation created enough space to respond differently. I said nothing for about ten seconds, made a neutral comment, and moved the conversation forward. Later I addressed it calmly with the person. It resolved without drama. What Vipassana gave me was not suppression ‚Äî I felt the reaction fully. It gave me the gap between feeling and acting.
Q: Have you failed to maintain equanimity despite Vipassana practice?
Yes, absolutely. During a particularly stressful preparation period, I had been irregular in my practice for nearly three weeks. A minor disagreement with a family member escalated into a much larger argument than it deserved. I said things with a sharpness that I recognized, even in the moment, was disproportionate. What the training did give me was the ability to recognize it quickly and course-correct within the same evening ‚Äî to go back, acknowledge what had happened, and repair the conversation without ego getting in the way of the apology. The real lesson was about consistency. Goenka always said ‚Äî skipping practice for weeks is like not eating for weeks and being surprised when you feel weak.
Q: Be honest ‚Äî what does your actual daily practice look like?
No, I do not sit for a full hour every single day. There have been stretches ‚Äî particularly during intense preparation phases ‚Äî where I missed days. Goenka recommends two one-hour sittings daily. I currently average one sitting of 30 to 45 minutes most mornings, with a shorter sitting in the evening when possible. What has been consistent is the morning practice. I protect morning sitting time almost regardless of what the day holds ‚Äî waking 45 minutes earlier to ensure the day begins with practice. Even on days when my formal sitting was minimal, I notice the difference in the quality of the day ‚Äî the level of reactivity, the clarity of attention. That difference itself is what sustains the motivation. Not discipline alone. Evidence.
Section 08 ‚Äî Anicca: Nothing Lasts Forever
Q: "Everything is temporary" ‚Äî why does hearing it in a meditation hall land differently than reading it in a book?
Because in a meditation hall ‚Äî you are not reading it. You are feeling it. There is a profound difference between knowing that nothing lasts forever and actually sitting with your hand on your knee, watching a sensation arise, intensify, and dissolve ‚Äî all within sixty seconds ‚Äî without doing a single thing to make it happen. The book gives you the map. The meditation hall takes you to the territory. The pain in your knee that felt like it would last forever ‚Äî shifts. The pleasant tingling that you wanted to hold ‚Äî fades. Everything demonstrated its own impermanence without being asked. And something that is demonstrated to you in your own body is qualitatively different from something you read. You cannot argue with it. It happened. You felt it. It changed you.
Q: If everything is temporary, doesn't that make life meaningless? Why work hard?
This is the most common misunderstanding ‚Äî that impermanence leads to nihilism or passivity. Understanding impermanence actually makes effort clean, not meaningless. Work toward a goal without clinging to the outcome ‚Äî and you work better. Your attention is fully on the work rather than split between the work and anxiety about the result. A surgeon analogy ‚Äî an attached surgeon panics when things don't go as expected. An equanimous surgeon adapts. Understanding that preparation, exam, and career are all temporary doesn't reduce motivation ‚Äî it frees you to give everything to the present moment without the self-defeating weight of compulsive attachment to outcome. Give completely, receive whatever comes with equanimity. That is complete engagement, not passivity.
Q: As a forest officer, how does "everything temporary" change your conservation approach?
Conservation is not about making permanent what is inherently impermanent. It is about not accelerating impermanence through careless human action. A forest ecosystem has natural temporariness ‚Äî species evolve, habitats shift over geological time. Conservation addresses the unnatural acceleration by exploitation and destruction. Anicca makes me a better conservationist by removing ego from the work. I won't save every forest, solve every conflict, or see the full fruits of what I plant. Knowing this frees me to do the work well without needing to see the outcome. A tree planted today will be fully grown 40 years after I have moved on ‚Äî plant it anyway, fully, without needing to hold the fruit. Officers who need immediate results burn out. Officers who understand their work as a thread in a larger tapestry serve well and long.
Q: What is the single most important thing "everything temporary" gave you?
The ability to begin. Before Vipassana ‚Äî paralysis before difficult things. The syllabus felt enormous and always would be. The uncertainty always real. The inadequacy a feature of who I was. All felt permanent. Understanding Anicca in the body, not just the head ‚Äî dissolved the permanence I had projected onto these things. The syllabus is large, yes ‚Äî but this sitting I can read this one chapter. The uncertainty is real, yes ‚Äî but this moment is already passing. The inadequacy is intense, yes ‚Äî but I have sat with intense sensations before and watched them change. Not comfort. Not optimism. Not reassurance. The ability to begin. Begin. Observe. Continue. ‚Äî the entire teaching in three words.
Section 09 ‚Äî Enlightenment
Q: What exactly is enlightenment in the context of Vipassana?
Goenka rarely used "enlightenment" directly ‚Äî the word has been so romanticized it has lost its actual meaning. He preferred the Pali term "Nibbana" ‚Äî which he described not as a place, not as a state of blissful floating ‚Äî but as the complete cessation of craving and aversion. An enlightened mind has completely uprooted the compulsive habit of reacting to experience with either wanting or not-wanting. Not the cessation of experience ‚Äî but the cessation of the compulsive, automatic, suffering-generating reaction to experience. He described it as the deepest peace imaginable. Not the peace of having everything you want. The peace of having been completely freed from the compulsion of wanting.
Q: Are you enlightened?
Obviously not. Not remotely. I am a beginner who has been practicing long enough to understand how much beginning remains. What I have is the practice. The direction. The genuine intention to keep walking. And the lived understanding ‚Äî verified in my own body and mind ‚Äî that walking this path produces something real, something useful, something that makes me a better person and I believe will make me a better officer. That is not enlightenment. But it is not nothing either. And I would rather offer you that honest answer than perform a spiritual achievement I have not reached.
Q: Is the pursuit of enlightenment selfish ‚Äî spending all this time on yourself?
The accusation assumes that inner work and outer service are in competition. They are not. The quality of useful action depends entirely on the quality of the mind performing it. A polluted mind performing charitable actions pollutes the actions. A reactive mind making policy decisions contaminates the policy. The time invested in cleaning the instrument is not selfish. It is the most fundamental form of preparation for genuine service. The world is better served by a person who has done this work than by one who has not. That is not a justification. That is a responsibility.
Section 10 ‚Äî Fad or Real? Science & Evidence
Q: Be honest ‚Äî do you genuinely believe meditation helps, or is it a modern fad?
Sir, I asked myself exactly this question before my first course. The skepticism was healthy. Because the meditation industry is genuinely full of fad. Expensive apps, celebrity endorsements, corporate mindfulness programs ‚Äî that deserves skepticism. But here is the distinction I had to make ‚Äî between the industry around meditation and the practice itself. The practice is 2500 years old. It predates every wellness trend, every self-help movement. It was developed not to make people more productive or relaxed, but to answer the most fundamental human question ‚Äî why do we suffer, and is there a way out? That question is not a fad. And the practice developed to answer it is not a fad either.
Q: What does science actually say about meditation?
The honest answer is ‚Äî both weak and strong research exists. Sara Lazar's work at Harvard using MRI imaging showed measurable structural changes in long-term meditators ‚Äî increased cortical thickness in the prefrontal cortex and reduced amygdala reactivity. The Tihar Jail experiment showed prisoners who completed 10-day courses had statistically significant reductions in aggression. Jon Kabat-Zinn's MBSR program has perhaps the largest clinical research base ‚Äî meta-analyses show consistent, moderate-to-large effect sizes for reducing anxiety, depression, and chronic pain. The evidence is strong enough that MBSR is now offered in NHS hospitals as a standard treatment option.
Q: Final question, hand on heart ‚Äî does meditation really help?
Sir, hand on heart ‚Äî yes. Not in the way the wellness industry promises. Not as a cure for anxiety or a shortcut to inner peace. But in the way that matters most ‚Äî It has made me more honest with myself. Before Vipassana ‚Äî I was capable of extraordinary self-deception. Of telling myself I was calm when I was afraid. Of believing I was acting from principle when I was acting from ego. Vipassana did not make these things stop. But it made them visible. And what is visible can be worked with. What is invisible runs the entire show without interference. That visibility ‚Äî that growing, imperfect, ongoing ability to see what is actually happening inside me ‚Äî is real. Is not a fad. Is perhaps the most useful thing I own. And it cost nothing except time and honesty. Which ‚Äî when you think about it ‚Äî is exactly what every true thing has always cost.
Section 11 ‚Äî IFoS Field Applications
Q: You are posted in a remote forest area with complete isolation for months. How does Vipassana help?
During the 10-day course, for the first three days, I had no phone, no books, no conversation. Initially the silence felt suffocating. But gradually, the silence became my companion rather than my enemy. By Day 5 or 6, I was genuinely comfortable being alone with myself ‚Äî something I had never truly experienced before. A remote forest posting is in many ways similar ‚Äî isolation, no urban comforts, limited social interaction. The officer who has never been alone with his own mind will crack under such conditions. Someone who has voluntarily sat with themselves for 10 days, without escape, knows that the human mind can find its own equilibrium if you stop fighting the situation.
Q: How does Vipassana help manage anger ‚Äî with poachers, encroachers, difficult staff?
Vipassana taught me something specific about anger ‚Äî it always has a physical component. There is a sensation in the body before the verbal or behavioral reaction. When you learn to observe that sensation rather than immediately act from it, the reaction loses its automatic quality. You don't become passive ‚Äî you become deliberate. In practical terms, when a poacher or encroacher confronts me aggressively, I now have the capacity to feel the provocation fully ‚Äî not suppress it ‚Äî but choose how to respond. Sometimes firm authority is needed. Sometimes calm dialogue achieves more. Vipassana gave me the clarity to choose the right tool for each situation rather than always defaulting to the same reactive pattern.
Q: How has Vipassana changed how you see nature and forests specifically?
One of the deepest experiences during my course was walking mindfully in the small garden during break time. Without the mental noise of constant thought and planning, I noticed things I had always walked past ‚Äî the texture of bark, the movement of a small insect, the way light changed through leaves within a single minute. My senses were genuinely sharper because the mental clutter had temporarily reduced. Vipassana teaches that all of existence operates by the same law of Anicca ‚Äî constant change. A forest is not a static resource ‚Äî it is a dynamic, breathing, interconnected system in constant flux. An officer who understands impermanence at a lived level will approach forest management with humility ‚Äî knowing that human intervention always has consequences we cannot fully predict.
Section 12 ‚Äî The Mosquito Answer ‚≠ê
‚≠ê This is your most memorable answer. Deliver it slowly. Let the silence after work.
Q: In one sentence ‚Äî what is Vipassana?
Vipassana is the art of not slapping the mosquito.
Q: Please explain ‚Äî what does that mean?
On Day 3 of my course, during a sitting, a mosquito landed on my arm. I could feel it moving, could feel the sting beginning. Every instinct said ‚Äî slap it. But the instruction was not to move for the duration of the sitting. So I stayed. And I watched. And within about a minute, the mosquito moved on by itself. I had done nothing.
